Chowder: A Dream of What Could Have Been

March 2026 — Boston, MA

Tucked away in the South End, Estella promised much and delivered a thick, tasteless nothing. 3/10.

Tucked away next to my fiancé and I's favorite restaurant — shout out to Hobgoblin — is another restaurant called Estella. It is unassuming from the outside. The inside, however, was quite beautifully decorated, probably like how most people's personalities are better than their outsides. The food was fine, although we decided we should have gotten lost and wandered into Hobgoblin instead. Granted, they have no chowder to please me.

However, this site is not about reviewing restaurants — it is about waxing poetic about the New England style Clam Chowders of the world. When I glanced at the menu, I saw my beloved Clam Chowder — for whomst I have not had the pleasure of purchasing in a while — and something lit up in me that is frankly disproportionate to the situation. This moment spawned a bit where I said I should start a blog about Clam Chowder. My fiancé, sitting directly across from me, threatened to break up with me if I did that. She does not know this blog exists. She cannot know. If you are reading this and you know her, I am begging you — as one mollusk friend to another — say nothing. This is between us and the clams. Anyways — the menu said Clam Chowder and I was like: fuck it, I make money, let's try it.

What really caught my eye was the fact that it didn't include bacon. When I see bacon in a clam chowder, I associate it with bad clam chowder — a sign of a kitchen that doesn't trust its own broth. We will get to that in another philosophy post later. I ordered. I was served a cup-sized clam chowder (for the price of a bowl — fourteen dollars) in a very well presented bowl. Which in my opinion is a bad start. Clam chowder is a meal of the people. It was born on fishing boats, eaten by people who pulled the clams out of the water themselves an hour before. It does not want a garnish. It does not want a pretty bowl. Dressing it up is almost an insult — like putting a tuxedo on a lobster trap.

We got the requisite crackers as well. The chowder had all the usual ingredients — clams (thank god), potatoes — but surprisingly had celery. Celery. You almost never see it anymore, which is a shame because it was in the original recipes and got quietly dropped somewhere along the way like an embarrassing family member. Estella had stumbled onto something more historically authentic and still managed to produce nothing. The chowder looked thick, which was a good start. On first taste, I was surprised, and sadly not impressed. While the chowder was thick, it had no taste. I took a second taste to confirm my suspicion. It was like drinking thick water — hard to describe, harder to explain to the person sitting across from you who is already questioning your life choices. The potatoes were cut squarely and cooked well, but cut quite large, which was a disappointment. The clams themselves were from a can, most likely, as most mollusks are at this point. The concoction itself had not a hint of delicious clam taste. The best part of the meal was the classic crackers — at least those had salt on them for some taste.

Anyways, this blog post will end as abruptly as my clam chowder from Estella — no fond remembrance, no dreams of going back, just nothing at all. 3/10 — would not recommend for my mollusk loving friends out there.
