Hugh O'Neill's: A Grievance

I write this piece in malice. Not due to bad chowder. Due to not chowder at all.

It was St. Paddy's day. 10am. I am an Englishman, and I was celebrating the day of my conquered people the only correct way — at an Irish bar, watching Ireland play Scotland in the rugby, drinking Guinness. Hugh O'Neill's in Malden. A bar I know well. A bar I have frequented. A bar I trusted.

They had clam chowder on the menu.

Much to my happiness.

Some friends and I drank passionately for a couple hours til food needed to be ingested. I, as any chowder minded man would do, ordered clam chowder. The bartender — and I want to be clear that I hold no personal grudge against this man, he was merely the messenger — claimed, with much sadness in his eyes, that they were out of it.

I sobbed. But recovered after a sip of Guinness and got fries instead. Fine. It was St. Paddy's day. These things happen. I was gracious about it. I moved on.

I came back a week later.

Specifically to order clam chowder. This was not a casual visit. This was a chowder pilgrimage. I looked on the menu.

Not there.

I asked the bartender. They took it off the menu recently. Due to not enough orders.

I sob.

Now I look like a fool and must ingest a chicken sandwich instead. A chicken sandwich. At an Irish bar. On my own time. Because I came here, on purpose, for chowder that no longer exists.

I write this piece in malice. Not due to bad chowder. Due to not chowder at all. This is the rare grievance with no resolution, no catharsis, no rating. You cannot rate an absence. You can only document it.

There is a side story I feel compelled to tell, because it belongs to this bar and this bar belongs to it.

Three years ago, when I frequented Hugh O'Neill's with some regularity, there was a Vietnam vet named Butch — odd that both bars in Malden had one, but that's Malden for you — who would always sit at the corner of the bar. Bud Light in hand. He even had his own salt shaker, labeled with his name on it, which he used to put salt in his beer. He said it gave him good minerals and improved the taste. I'm not sure if that's true.

He's gone now. Where, I don't know. Hopefully not to the grave. Butch gave the bar a certain character and was happy to share tales both happy and sad.

The chowder is gone. Butch is gone. I got a chicken sandwich.

Watch this space. If Hugh O'Neill's brings the chowder back, I will return. I will order it. And I will report back honestly and without malice.
