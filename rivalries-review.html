Rivalries: Thin, Thin, Thin

May 2026 — Falmouth, ME

The chowder was thin. The pork was undercooked. The potatoes were, inexplicably, perfect. 4/10.

There is a theory — and I believe it — that the sports bar exists in a kind of philosophical tension with good food. The game is the thing. The game is always the thing. The food is incidental, a concession to the biological reality that human beings need to eat and cannot survive on draft beer alone, legally. And so the food arrives as an afterthought, engineered to be acceptable rather than good, present rather than correct. This is the original sin of the sports pub and it does not have to be this way.

Clam chowder in particular is a meal of the people. It is working-class food. It is what fishermen ate when they came in from the water, cold and hungry, needing something that would fill them and warm them and make them feel like the world was survivable. It was not precious. It was not garnished. It was made right because making it right was not hard — cream, clams, potatoes, pork, salt — and because the people eating it deserved to be fed well. A sports bar is full of those people. Cold, loud, needing something that makes the world feel survivable. There is no cuisine on earth more appropriate for the environment. And yet.

The chowder was thin. Thin. Thin. Not thin as in delicate, not thin as in it had the grace of a consommé — thin as in it had somewhere to be and didn't stop to thicken. Clam water with aspirations.

The pork was undercooked. I don't know how to say this more plainly. The pork. Was undercooked. In a chowder review you expect to have opinions about cream viscosity and clam-to-potato ratios, and yet here we are talking about undercooked pork.

The potatoes, though. The potatoes were perfectly cooked — and I mean that without irony, they were genuinely the platonic ideal of a chowder potato, holding their shape, giving when pressed, doing everything a potato is supposed to do. Those potatoes deserved to be in a better chowder and I hope they know that.

Taste was minimal. More flavor than a void — credit where it's due — but it needed salt. Badly.

The kitchen at Rivalries can cook a potato. Someone back there knows what they're doing. They just didn't apply it to the chowder. The chowder was an afterthought. It doesn't have to be.

4/10. The potatoes were good. That's the whole thing. That's where we are.
