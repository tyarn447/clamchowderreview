Black Rose: The Vat Has Tides

May 2026 — Boston, MA

Back at Black Rose with two old friends and live Irish music. I vouched for the chowder — hard, on record, to someone who trusted me. The vat was not at peak. 6.5/10.

Some people fade from your life slowly — no fight, no moment, just distance and time and the way schedules stop overlapping — and then suddenly they're back and it's like nothing happened and also like everything happened. Both at once. Whiplash. This is the nature of it.

Two old friends. A Tuesday. Black Rose.

The music was going when we walked in, which is always a good sign. Live Irish music at Black Rose on a good night is one of the better things this city has to offer — it doesn't ask you to pay attention, it just takes it — and tonight was a good night. Music does to me what chowder does. Both need to be real more than they need to be perfect.

I vouched for the chowder. I need you to understand what I mean by this. I had been here before. I gave it a 10/10 — the best I'd had in years, a flavor I couldn't do justice to in the words of our simple English language, etc. I told my friend this. He was convinced. We ordered.

My face fell.

I kept it together outwardly. But internally: that small private grief. The creaminess was there — good, maybe great, thick and right, held its shape when you dragged the spoon through it. But creaminess was all it was. The flavor was somewhere behind the cream, not gone but not showing up. The clam was in another room. A shadow of the thing I had remembered and promised.

My theory is rush hour. We were there early evening, kitchen churning fast, the vat not at its freshest pull. This is a real phenomenon — I believe in the vat, I have always believed in the vat, but the vat has tides. You want to catch it right.

My friend said it was good. He was being polite. I had told this man, with genuine conviction, that this was the best chowder I had in years — had built it up, had been the guy who recommended it, had staked something on it — and what arrived was fine. Politely fine. The embarrassment of the evangelist when the miracle doesn't come through.

We closed the night with a pint of Guinness. This fixes most things. The music was still going. My friends were still there.

6.5/10. Come back. Catch it at high tide.
